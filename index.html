Searching the Web

The Question

I have never thought much of a search bar component that I interact with daily - whether it's YouTube, Wiki, Google Drive or Twitch. Everything now has a search bar and it felt straightforward to use from day one. However, I also realized that I never experienced a mismatch in its design: I am a daily mouse and keyboard user. If you remove one of them, how hard can it get? My intuition told me that it shouldn't be a problem, so today, we will explore how accessible a simple search bar is.

Input checking

The first part of the experiment is to try out the search bars accross different environments. I chose the ones that I use most often, Google Drive, YouTube, and Twitch TV searches. To measure their accessability, I gave all of them a go with using primarely mouse/touchpad, keyboard, or a touchscreen. My main focus points were general component behavior, capabilities, how easy it is to learn and use, and how accessible it felt. You can find the report in the table on the other side.

Component Redesign

One particular point of frustration during the screen reader usage was attempting to get to dynamic recommendations based on the query. Pressing tab would sometimes skip it, and arrow keys could refuse to work, even if prompts are on the screen. Perhaps, the design of the page does not provide something for screen reader/tab system to "attach to". My proposal: create a hardcoded recommendation line, that's a part of the search bar. This way, the "recommendations" are always there, and screen reader code can flow from one part of the search bar (the actual search) to the recommendations window, without skipping to the next element.

Reflections on Inclusive Design

This part is inspired by the video Kat Holmes' Inclusive Design.

Usability and accessability

Google Drive was the only search bar that failed dragging text into the box or right clicking on the bar and pasting the text. I would make sure to use a system that at least supports dragging the text into the search bar or pasting by using the mouse. Ideally, I would take YouTube’s idea and place a button that opens a mini keyboard on the screen that you could use with a mouse.

Acessability considerations in my design

The screen reader was a pretty daunting experience across the board, mostly because of its cryptic messaging and component order. In my design, I tried to make it simple so that the screen reader doesn’t get overwhelmed, but also added a small component that would help the screen reader be more organized.

Solving for a mismatch

This particular mismatch is defined as a mismatch between human interactions and design of the application. A mismatch that I accounted for is the idea that most users are able to see the search bar recommendations results, which is not always true, and screen reader is supposed to help with that!

Positive and Negative Scenarios

If a user with a visual impairment would try to use YouTube with a screen reader, they would quickly find it nice that using tab gets them to the search bar fairly quickly, which is a positive experience. However, if they would try to use the screen reader to navigate to recommendations of the their current query, they could find it extremely frustrating to find the dropdown list without seeing it, which is a negative experience.

Who is the most commonly prioritized?

It is easy to understand that the most commonly prioritized components are either touch screen (for mobile apps) or mouse/keyboard users. Most people use a searchbar either on their phone or on a laptop, where both the keyboard and mouse are built-in to the machine. Therefore, for the average user, designers and engineers are not required to think about what happens when one or the other are inaccessible.
